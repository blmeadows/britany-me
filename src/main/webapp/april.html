Functional Programming

For my first post I want to start a collection of resources I have come across that help to describe and explain functional programming. Last summer I accepted an internship position at Mentor Graphics, and my team lead, Joe Barnes, suggested a MOOC (Massive Open Online Course) to help prepare me and get my feet wet with Scala and the functional programming paradigm in general. He recommended I start with Functional Programming Principles in Scala, a course instructed by Martin Odersky, designer of the Scala language, on Coursera. This course was my first exposure to functional programming in any way.

In the last nine or so months, I feel like I have been exposed to and experienced "a whole new world." I have fallen in love with FP (functional programming). But along the way I've really had to learn what exactly is functional programming. Not only for my own understanding, but I've had conversations with my classmates, with typically the same experience as I had before, and needed to be able to properly explain my new found discovery of awesomeness. Discussion also began in the monthly functional meet-up group, HuntFunc, on how to have meaningful conversations with others new to the functional programming paradigm.

I will come back to more language specific resources in a moment. Each language has its pros and cons, but we need to understand the defining characteristics of this usually unfamiliar paradigm before we try to compare the various tools that try to execute it. Kris Jenkins wrote two great posts in December on the topic. Part 1: What is Functional Programming? and Part 2: Which Programming Languages are Functional?. These two are my new favorite posts and emphasise how in the FP world complexity and side-effects are evil and the importance of avoiding them and striving for more pure functions.

Programming languages can be typically viewed on a scale of how functional it is. Scala is considered a hybrid language, that allows for object-oriented and functional paradigms, and is often looked at as a bridge from OO (Java, in particular) to FP. From the Scala world, the courses that I mentioned earlier (and a few more) are being transitioned to the new ScalaCenter, which will soon offer a Scala mini-degree offered on Coursera. On the paradigm scale, Haskell is located closer to the functional side. Although I have yet to finish this ebook, Christopher Allen and Julie Moronuki have introduced Haskell Programming from First Principles, and it is looking very promising so far.

I think this will be enough of my rambling for my first blog post. I think I may delve further into the various programming languages that are closer to the functional programming side of the paradigm scale next time and discuss various differences they have, such as static or dynamic typing. Thanks for reading!
